Computing the expecation within the typical set

The ultimate undertaking of statistical computing is evaluating the expectation

Eπ[f]=∫Qπ(q)f(q)dq\mathbb{E}_\pi[f]=\int_Q\pi(q)f(q)dqEπ​[f]=∫Q​π(q)f(q)dq

Analytically computing this integral is impossible and we must approximate it with limited computational resources.

Parsimonious computing of the expecation may just focus around the mode of the target dist. but the volume of the mode may be extremely small in high-dimensional space. The massive volunme at the tail of the dist. may contribute a lot to the expecation despite the small density.

To keep a balance between density and volunme, we need to find the typical set that is between the two extremes. Outside the typical set, π(q)dq\pi(q)dqπ(q)dq is generally neglectable.

MCMC to explore the typical set

A MC is a progression of points by sequentially applying a random transition T(q′∣q)T(q'|q)T(q′∣q). If the transition preserves the target dist.

π(q)=∫Qπ(q′)T(q∣q′)dq′\pi(q) = \int_Q \pi(q')T(q|q')dq'π(q)=∫Q​π(q′)T(q∣q′)dq′

then the markov chain will converge towards the typical set.

Given a certain amount of time, the histroy of the markov chain becomes a convenient quantification of the typical set, and the expecation is simply

f^N=1N∑n=0Nf(qn)lim⁡N→∞f^N=Eπ[f]\hat{f}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^N f(q_n) \quad \lim_{N\to \infty}\hat{f}_N = \mathbb{E}_\pi[f]f^​N​=N1​n=0∑N​f(qn​)N→∞lim​f^​N​=Eπ​[f]

The MH Algorithm

The MH algo is comprised of two steps, a proposal and a correction. The proposal is any kind of stochastic perturbation and the correction rejects any proposal that strays too far away from the typical set. Let Q(q′∣q)Q(q'|q)Q(q′∣q) be the pdf that defines the proposal, then the probability of accepting the proposal is

a(q′∣q)=min⁡(1,Q(q∣q′)π(q′)Q(q′∣q)π(q))a(q'|q)=\min\left(1, \frac{Q(q|q')\pi(q')}{Q(q'|q)\pi(q)}\right)a(q′∣q)=min(1,Q(q′∣q)π(q)Q(q∣q′)π(q′)​)

The random walk metroplis is simply using a Gaussian noise for the proposal,

Q(q′∣q)=N(q,Σ)Q(q'|q)=\mathcal{N}(q, \Sigma)Q(q′∣q)=N(q,Σ)

As the proposal is symmetric, the acceptance is therefore just

a(q′∣q)=min⁡(1,π(q′)π(q))a(q'|q)=\min\left(1, \frac{\pi(q')}{\pi(q)}\right)a(q′∣q)=min(1,π(q)π(q′)​)

However, in high dimensions, the volume outside the typical set is overwhelming and therefore the acceptance ratio of the random walk could vanish and makes the MC rarely moves. One can shrink the size of the proposal to stay inside the typical set, but that could makes the MC moves really slowly in exploring the typical set.

Intuition of HMC

In order to make large jumps from the inital points and into new unexplored regions of the typical set, we need to exploit the geometry information of the typical set. Specifically, we need transitions that can follow the contours of high probability mass.

Intuitively, the probabilistic endeavor of exploring the typical set is equivalent to placing a satellite in a stable orbit around the hypothetical planet. Although the objects at rest will crash into the planet, just like the samples will fall into the mode if we naively follow the gradient, we can matain the stable orbit by endowing the satellite with just enough momentum to counteract the gravitational attraction.

In order to mimic this behavior in the probabilistic system, we need to introduce auxiliary momentum parameters pnp_npn​ and expand the DDD dimensional parameter space to 2D2D2D, qn→(pn,qn)q_n \to (p_n, q_n)qn​→(pn​,qn​), and also expand the target distribution into a canonical distribution,

π(p,q)=π(p∣q)π(q)\pi(p,q)=\pi(p|q)\pi(q)π(p,q)=π(p∣q)π(q)

As the canonical distribution does not depend on any specific parameterization, we can write it in terms of an invariant Hamiltonian function

π(p,q)=e−H(p,q)\pi(p,q)=e^{-H(p,q)}π(p,q)=e−H(p,q)

which we can call energy due to the physical analogy.

Because of the factorization of the joint density, the Hamiltonian

H(p,q)=−log⁡π(p,q)=−log⁡π(p∣q)−log⁡π(q)=K(p,q)+V(p,q)H(p,q) = -\log \pi(p,q) \\ = -\log \pi(p|q) - \log \pi(q) \\ = K(p,q) + V(p,q)H(p,q)=−logπ(p,q)=−logπ(p∣q)−logπ(q)=K(p,q)+V(p,q)

where K(p,q)K(p,q)K(p,q) is called the kinetic energy, corresponding to the auxiliary momentum distribution. V(p,q)V(p,q)V(p,q) is called the potential energy, corresponding to the target distribution. Note that the kinetic distribution is unconstrained and must be specified by the implementation.

The desired vector field oriented with the typical set can be derived from the hamiltonian equations,

dqdt=∂H∂p=∂K∂p+∂V∂p\frac{dq}{dt}=\frac{\partial H}{\partial p} = \frac{\partial K}{\partial p}+ \frac{\partial V}{\partial p}dtdq​=∂p∂H​=∂p∂K​+∂p∂V​
dpdt=−∂H∂q=−∂K∂q−∂V∂q\frac{dp}{dt}=-\frac{\partial H}{\partial q} = -\frac{\partial K}{\partial q} - \frac{\partial V}{\partial q}dtdp​=−∂q∂H​=−∂q∂K​−∂q∂V​

Note that ∂V∂p=0\frac{\partial V}{\partial p}=0∂p∂V​=0 and ∂V∂q\frac{\partial V}{\partial q}∂q∂V​ is the gradient of the log target distribution. We see that Hamilton’s equations twist differential information to align with the typical set of canonical distribution by channeling the gradient through the momentum instead of the target parameters directly.

Following the Hamiltonian vector field for some time ttt generates the trajectory ϕt(p,q)\phi_t(p,q)ϕt​(p,q) and projecting the trajectory back to the target parameter space will yields the efficient exploration of the target typical set.

The Ideal HMC Transition

We need to introduce momentum to a given point in the target distribution. We can simply lift a point in the parameter space to the phase space by sampling from

p∼π(p∣q)p \sim \pi(p|q)p∼π(p∣q)

assuming the inital point is in the typical set, we then can propogate by

(q,p)→ϕt(q,p)(q,p) \to \phi_t(q,p)(q,p)→ϕt​(q,p)

and finally, we can return to the parameter space by projecting away the momentum

(q,p)→q(q,p) \to q(q,p)→q

Optimized Implementation of HMC

The entire HMC chain decouples into two phases: deterministic exploration of individual level sets and stochastic exploration across the level sets.

Choice of Kinetc Energy

The choice of the kinetic energy function, which is the conditional distribution of the mementum, completes the probability structure on phase space and then determines the geometry of the microcanonical decomposition. The ideal choice will make the energy level choice as uniform as possible and the conditional energy transition distribution match the marginal energy distribution as well as possible.

Euclidean-Gaussian: the most common choice of HMC.

and

Other momentum distributions inlcude Rieman-Gaussian and Non-Gaussian* families.

Choice of Integration Time

The kinetic energy decides the geometry and the shape of the energy levels, but how effectively each HMC trajectory explore those level sets is then determined by the choice of integration time of the phase space T(q,p)T(q,p)T(q,p). Intuitively, if the time is short, then we do not take full advantage of the coherent exploration of HMC. If the time is too long, then it is possible that the trajectory returns to previously explored neighborhoods.
